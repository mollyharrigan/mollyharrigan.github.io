From the melting of the polar ice caps to an increase in global natural disasters, for years, scientists and politicians alike have acknowledged the concept of climate change and how detrimental it can and will be years down the line. Yet, we continue pushing the matter aside, handling "more pressing issues" first. However, numerous studies have shown the impact of climate change and how it's already affecting our planet, demonstrating the necessity for instant action and intervention. This is no longer a concept of the distant future, this is a conflict of the now.

With extensive evidence suggesting that greenhouse gasses in the Earth's atmosphere, caused by an influx of fossil fuel emissions, are rapidly increasing Earth's annual average temperatures, it's no surprise that farmers are the most-likely group noticing these initial signs of a bigger problem. With crop rotation being essential for not only farmers to maintain a steady income but also critical for supplying large-scale production companies certain resources needed to produce many day-to-day goods and household items, this increase in temperature and overall warming of what should be the cooler months of the year, is causing crop yields to drop as temperatures rise. Many large farms have begun crop-switching, a process intended to mitigate the impacts of climate change on agriculture and is where farmers begin substituting colder-weather crops for warmer-weather ones. Despite this seeming like a productive plan of action, this can actually exacerbate the climate change crisis as a whole. Taking a more niche stance, we can see direct changes solely to the New York State region and its climate from 2015-2020 alone.

Climate change Professor Daniel Curewitz of Syracuse University shared in response to crop switching, "Let's say I'm a farmer. Corn doesn't grow anymore so now I'm going to grow soy. Well now nobody is growing corn. And then what? You've done nothing to change the overall system which means in ten years, you can't grow soy anymore either, you have to grow cactuses. Pushing it down the road, winds up making it worse later."

There is a clear trend in the data, reflecting as New York State average temperatures increase, crop yields decrease, something directly refuting ideas that there are no present-day indicators of climate change and its impacts. In short, this crisis is becoming more and more prevalent and visible in the present day and is something that cannot be further dismissed. If we continue in our industrial-driven ways without implication alternate, more adaptive measures, we are only further exacerbating the problem. It's clear to most what the root of the problem is and in order to move forwards in efforts to reverse the already established damage, we must nip it in the bud.

Professor Curewitz continues, "The further this problem gets from being addressed, the more effort we are going to have to put into solving this or we're finished. Big systems and big problems require big changes and the reality is, it's very difficult to simply reverse this stuff. In scientific terms, what we say is that, unburning the smoke requires more energy than you got from the original fire. We have to stop fueling the original fire."

From 2015-2020, the average temperature per year across the state of New York increased by nearly 2 degrees Fahrenheit while crop yields decreased by nearly half a million, mirroring global trends. Together these findings reveal a shocking trend that the rest of the United States and the globe are currently facing, emphasizing the lack of security our food sources now have in relation to planet warming. Many are quick to dismiss climate change concerns, not recogniing the severity of its impacts and how they too are and will be directly impacted. Conditions influenced by planet warming will inevitably kill our food supply, our water supply, and our ability to function in the ways our society is currently doing.
